Climate Change Overview

Understanding the science, causes, effects, and implications

The Science

What is Climate Change?

Climate change refers to long-term shifts in global temperatures and weather patterns. While climate variability has occurred naturally throughout Earth's history, current warming trends are driven by human activities.

The scientific consensus is overwhelming: 97% of climate scientists agree that climate-warming trends over the past century are due to human activities, primarily through emissions of greenhouse gases from burning fossil fuels.

Root Factors

Causes of Climate Change

01

Fossil Fuel Combustion

Burning coal, oil, and natural gas releases massive amounts of CO₂, accounting for about 75% of emissions.

02

Deforestation

Cutting down forests eliminates crucial carbon sinks that absorb CO₂ from the atmosphere.

03

Industrial Processes

Manufacturing and cement production emit CO₂ and other greenhouse gases through production.

04

Transportation

Vehicles, airplanes, and ships contribute significantly through combustion of petroleum-based fuels.

05

Agriculture

Livestock farming produces methane, while rice cultivation and fertilizer use release greenhouse gases.

06

Waste Management

Decomposing organic waste in landfills produces methane, compounding the climate crisis.

Consequences

Effects of Climate Change

Rising Temperatures

Average global temperatures continue to rise, leading to more frequent and intense heatwaves affecting health and agriculture.

Melting Ice & Rising Seas

Polar ice caps and glaciers are melting rapidly. Sea levels have risen about 8 inches since 1900.

Extreme Weather

More frequent hurricanes, storms, floods, droughts, and wildfires devastate communities worldwide.

Ecosystem Disruption

Species face habitat loss and extinction risks as ecosystems struggle to adapt to rapid changes.

Ocean Changes

Oceans are warming and acidifying, threatening marine life including coral reefs and fisheries.

Food & Water Security

Changing patterns disrupt agriculture, threatening food production and freshwater availability.

STS Framework

Analyzing Through STS Lens

01

Social Issues & Impacts

Climate change is fundamentally a social justice issue, disproportionately affecting vulnerable populations.

Global Inequality

The poorest countries contribute least to emissions but suffer most from climate impacts.

Health Disparities

Low-income communities face higher exposure to pollution, heat stress, and climate-related diseases.

Forced Migration

Environmental degradation and extreme weather are displacing millions, creating climate refugees.

02

Technological Issues & Solutions

Technology plays a dual role—as both a driver through fossil fuel use and as a potential solution.

Renewable Energy

Solar, wind, and hydroelectric technologies offer clean alternatives with improving efficiency.

Carbon Capture

Emerging technologies aim to remove CO₂ from the atmosphere, though challenges remain.

Green Innovation

New technologies in sustainable agriculture and circular economy offer sustainable pathways.

03

Ethical Issues & Responsibilities

Climate change raises profound ethical questions about justice and our obligations to future generations.

Intergenerational Justice

Current generations benefit from fossil fuels while imposing costs on future generations.

Historical Responsibility

Developed nations industrialized first—should they bear greater responsibility for solutions?

Climate Justice

Climate action must address existing inequalities rather than perpetuating them.
